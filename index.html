Introduction

There is a crucial interrelation between microstructure and chemical, physical, and/or mechanical properties of materials.

Digital imaging techniques are becoming a fast growing method for microstructural characterization. Since there is a crucial interrelation between the microstructure and the chemical, physical, and/or mechanical properties of a material, one is obliged to examine them well, if one wants to understand the macroscopical features of a material.

There are three aspects of microstructural characterization:

  1. The different crystallographic phases
  2. The morphology of these phases
  3. The local chemical composition and variations in chemical composition

In this course:

Characterization of the microstructure both qualitative and quantitative, as well as chemical composition.

In this course, we will explore the digital characterization of the microstructure at both qualitative level and quantitative level, as well as some techniques to examine the chemical composition.

  • X-rays
  • beam of high energy electrons

The microstructural and chemical characterization relies mostly on the interaction of a material with a probe. This probe is usually visible light, X-rays or a beam of high energy electrons. The resultant signal will then be collected, processed and interpreted.

Visible light:

Microscopy: allows looking at objects not visible for the naked eye.

Limitation: the smallest distinguishable features are determined by the wavelength of visual light (0.4 - 0.7 μm)

Microscopy has always been an intriguing area of science, since it allows looking at objects in a way which is beyond the possibilities of the naked eye. From the end of the 16th century, optical lenses were used to magnify objects more than a hundredfold, which opened a whole new world to scientists of all fields. Although modern optical microscopes make it possible to look at matter at the microscopic scale, there is also a limit. The smallest distinguishable features are determined by the wavelength of visual light (±0.4 - 0.7 μm), due to the fact that even a microscope with perfect lenses is subjected to diffractive effects. However, by changing the source of illumination to a radiation probe with a smaller wavelength, smaller details can be observed.

Overview of various types of EM-radiation and their wavelength

Smaller wavelength than visible light => smaller details can be observed

Electrons can be accelerated up to a velocity near the speed of light => electrons with a wavelength ≈ 0.1 nm => atomic and molecular scale of matter.

Figure 1 gives an overview of various types of electromagnetic radiation and corresponding real world objects with a size similar to their wavelength. Since electrons can be accelerated up to a velocity very close to the speed of light, it is possible to create electrons with a wavelength down to 0.1 nm, enabling scientists do go down to the atomic and molecular scale of matter.

Electron microscopes:

Electrons emitted from a heated filament are accelerated by a high voltage and then focused using magnetic lenses onto a sample, resolution in these devices is limited by lenses.

In electron microscopes, a high voltage is used to accelerate electrons emitted from a heated filament, which can then be focused using magnetic lenses onto a sample. The resolution in these devices is limited by the lenses.

One issue which is common to both microscopic techniques is the need for sample preparation. Because of the limited penetrating power of both visible light and electrons, thin slices of the sample have to be produced to investigate the sample in transmission (the amount of transmission through the sample gives information about the composition). It is also possible to study objects by detecting reflected light/particles, but even then some kind of sample preparation (like polishing) is often needed. In both modes, no true 3D information can be obtained (only slices or surfaces are visualised). Furthermore, these methods are often destructive, making it impossible to perform other analytical techniques on the same sample afterwards.

X-rays:

X-rays typically have a wavelength below 10 nm => in practice resolution is limited because of other reasons (focusing devices).

Their penetrating power is largely dependent on their energy: The energy of an X-ray is reversely proportional to its wavelength and is calculated by the equation E=hc/lambda

X-rays are an alternative probe to study small objects. As can be seen in Figure 1, X-rays typically have a wavelength below 10 nm, which means that in practice the resolution is limited because of other reasons (focusing devices). Another important fact about X-rays is that they are attenuated less easily than electrons and visible light. More importantly, their penetrating power is largely dependent on their energy (which is directly related to their wavelength), making it possible to adjust the energy of the source to the type of sample under investigation (based on size, composition,...).

X-rays are not only for microscopic purposes (µm size), but in general provides a method for non-destructive imaging of objects of all sizes (up to meters).

This makes X-rays not only suitable for microscopic purposes, but in general provides a method for non-destructive imaging of objects of all sizes (from several meters large down to only a few micrometers).

Some general terms:

  • Macrostructure: Those features visible to the naked eye. Ex: Large pores, big inclusions, cracks.
  • Mesostructure: those features that are on the borderline of the visible.
  • Microstructure: covers the scale of structural phenomena most commonly of concern to geologists, namely grain and particle size, microcracking and microporosity.
  • Nanostructure: restricted to sub-micrometre features including the width of grain boundaries. A nanostructure is an object of intermediate size between microscopic and molecular structures.

Since “microstructure” is a very general term, used to cover a wide range of structural features from those visible to the naked eye down to those corresponding to the interatomic distances in the crystal lattice, it is good practice to distinguish between macrostructure, mesostructure, microstructure and nanostructure.

  • Macrostructure refers to those features visible to the naked eye. Examples include large pores, big inclusions, shrinkage cracks, etc.
  • Mesostructure is a less common term, but is useful to describe those features that are on the borderline of the visible.
  • Microstructure covers the scale of structural phenomena most commonly of concern to geologists, namely grain and particle size, microcracking and microporosity.
  • Nanostructure is restricted to sub-micrometre features including the width of grain boundaries. A nanostructure is an object of intermediate size between microscopic and molecular structures.

Applications in Geology

link between nano-, micro and macroscale processes inside porous material

During this course, several techniques including the analysis of digital thin sections (largely based on the book of John Russ “The imaging processing handbook”) obtained by optical microscopy, scanning electron microscopy techniques, X-ray CT, neutron CT and XRF will be discussed.

A high quality petrographical microscope can usefully magnify up to 600 times with a maximum resolution of around 1 µm. Where closer examination is required, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) is invaluable as it achieves significantly higher magnifications up to 50.000 times and resolutions of around 7 nm, by using a beam of high-energy electrons to replace the light of conventional microscopes. In addition to their high-resolution capabilities, electron microscopes have great depth of field, producing electron images that have a 3D effect. By combining SEM with an electron probe microanalysis system (EPM) elemental chemical analysis of characteristic X-ray spectra emitted by the sample can be performed. By performing “spot” analysis on selected points of the sample, a semi-quantitative elemental analysis can be performed, while “elemental mapping” scans along a square line raster over a larger area of the sample surface allow the observation of variations in elemental distributions.

Mineralogical analysis by X-ray diffraction (XRD) can also be useful for identification of crystalline minerals when the optical properties do not allow definitive microscopical identification. XRD (which is not in the scope of this course) has the advantage of actually identifying the minerals present, although estimation of the relative amounts is semi-quantitative. Other spectroscopic analysis methods such as X-ray fluorescence (XRF), atomic absorption (AAS), and inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectroscopy (ICP-AES) give very precise determinations of elemental composition. However, the elemental composition data from these techniques require skilled interpretation by the analyst to identify the proportions of mineral phases present.

X-ray fluorescence (XRF) is the emission of characteristic "secondary" (or fluorescent) X-rays from a material that has been excited by bombarding with high-energy X-rays or gamma rays. The phenomenon is widely used for chemical analysis. Atomic absorption spectroscopy (AAS) is a spectroanalytical procedure for the quantitative determination of chemical elements employing the absorption of optical radiation (light) by free atoms in the gaseous state. In analytical chemistry, the technique is used for determining the concentration of a particular element in a sample to be analyzed. Inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectroscopy (ICP-AES), also referred to as inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectrometry (ICP-OES), is an analytical technique used for the detection of trace metals. It is a type of emission spectroscopy that uses the inductively coupled plasma to produce excited atoms and ions that emit electromagnetic radiation at wavelengths characteristic of a particular element (Stefánsson et al., 2007). The intensity of this emission is indicative of the concentration of the element within the sample.

High-resolution X-ray Computed Tomography (HRXCT) or micro-CT (µCT) is a frequently used non-destructive 3D imaging and analysis technique for the investigation of internal structures of a large variety of objects, including geomaterials. One of the main advantages of this technique is the fact that it is a non-destructive characterization technique which allows 4D monitoring of internal structural changes at resolutions down to a few hundred nanometres. X-ray CT, just like XRF, can be performed both in laboratory environment or at synchrotron facilities. Neutron CT is a form of computed tomography involving the production of three-dimensional images by the detection of the absorbance of neutrons produced by a neutron source. It creates a three-dimensional image of an object by combining multiple planar images with a known separation just like X-ray CT. Whilst its resolution is lower than that of X-ray tomography, it can be useful for specimens containing low contrast between the matrix and object of interest; for instance, fossils with a high carbon content, such as plants or vertebrate remains or water inside stone. However, neutron tomography can have the unfortunate side-effect of leaving imaged samples radioactive.

Stefánsson A, Gunnarsson I, Giroud N (2007). "New methods for the direct determination of dissolved inorganic, organic and total carbon in natural waters by Reagent-Free Ion Chromatography and inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectrometry". Anal. Chim. Acta 582 (1): 69–74.

What are "digital images"?

Digital images

Electronic snapshots taken of a scene or scanned from documents, sampled and mapped as a grid of dots or picture elements (pixels).

DIGITAL IMAGES are electronic snapshots taken of a scene or scanned from documents, such as photographs or manuscripts. The digital image is sampled and mapped as a grid of dots or picture elements (pixels).

Pixels

In digital imaging, a pixel, or picture element is a physical point in a raster image, or the smallest addressable element in an all points addressable display device, so it is the smallest controllable element of a picture represented on the screen.

Each pixel is assigned a tonal value (black, white, shades of gray or colour), which is represented in binary code. The binary digits ("bits") for each pixel are stored in a sequence by a computer and often reducedto a mathematical representation (compressed). The bits are then interpreted and read by the computer to produce an analog version for display or printing.

Pixel values: As shown in this bitonal image, each pixel is assigned a tonal value, in this example 0 for black and 1 for white.

Each pixel is assigned a tonal value (black, white, shades of gray or colour), which is represented in binary code (zeros and ones) (fig. 1). The binary digits ("bits") for each pixel are stored in a sequence by a computer and often reduced to a mathematical representation (compressed). The bits are then interpreted and read by the computer to produce an analog version for display or printing.

Resolution

Resolution is the ability to distinguish fine spatial detail.

The spatial frequency at which a digital image is sampled (the sampling frequency) is often a good indicator of resolution.

Dots-per-inch (dpi) or pixels-per-inch (ppi) are common and synonymous terms used to express resolution for digital images.

RESOLUTION is the ability to distinguish fine spatial detail. The spatial frequency at which a digital image is sampled (the sampling frequency) is often a good indicator of resolution. This is why dots-per-inch (dpi) or pixels-per-inch (ppi) are common and synonymous terms used to express resolution for digital images (fig. 3). Generally, but within limits, increasing the sampling frequency also helps to increase resolution; the more pixels are present in a given area, the higher the resolution of the image.

the more pixels an image contains, the more it can be enlarged before the separate pixels start to show

If a digital image is enlarged there will come a point when the individual elements can be seen as separate dots (fig. 2-3) - the more pixels an image contains, the more it can be enlarged before the separate pixels start to show.

Resolution is perhaps the most important parameter in 3D imaging techniques, as well as for for example, the selection of the camera. Images must be able to record the fine detail revealed by the microscope magnification. Resolution is also often quoted as the size of charged coupled device (CCD), which is effectively the number of pixels on the chip. It should be noted, however, that the size of the individual pixels varies amongst different types of CCDs. For microscopy, a pixel size (square) of 6.7 micrometres is thought to be ideal. Increasing resolution enables the capture of finer detail. At some point, however, added resolution will not result in an appreciable gain in image quality, only larger file size. The key is to determine the resolution necessary to capture all significant detail present in the source document.

Bit depth

= determined by the number of bits used to define each pixel.

Digital images

  • black and white (bitonal)
  • greyscale

BIT DEPTH is determined by the number of bits used to define each pixel. The greater the bit depth, the greater the number of tones (grayscale or colour) that can be represented. Digital images may be produced in black and white (bitonal), grayscale, or colour. An elevation map of the earth’s surface encoded with 8 bits (256 values) spread over the range from sea level to the top of Mt. Everest corresponds to about 30,5 m per bit and would not show most of Florida as distinct from sea level. With 2 bytes per pixel (65,536 values) each bit represents about 0,15 m and the map can distinguish the curbs along most streets. With 4 bytes per pixel (4 billion values) each bit corresponds to less than 200 μm, and the roughness of sand on a beach can be recorded.

Bitonal image

  • bit depth = 1
  • pixels can represent two tones (typically black and white):

A bitonal image is represented by pixels consisting of 1 bit each, which can represent two tones (typically black and white), using the values 0 for black and 1 for white or vice versa (Fig. 6).

Greyscale image

  • bit depth > 1
  • typically from 2 to 8 bits or more.

A grayscale image is composed of pixels represented by multiple bits of information, typically ranging from 2 to 8 bits or more (Fig. 6). Example: In a 2-bit image, there are four possible combinations: 00, 01, 10, and 11. If "00" represents black, and "11" represents white, then "01" equals dark gray and "10" equals light gray. The bit depth is two, but the number of tones that can be represented is 2 2 or 4. At 8 bits, 256 (2 8) different tones can be assigned to each pixel.

Colour images

A colour image is typically represented by a bit depth ranging from 8 to 24 or higher. With a 24-bit image, the bits are often divided into three groupings: 8 for red, 8 for green, and 8 for blue. Combinations of those bits are used to represent other colours. A 24-bit image offers 16.7 million (2 24) colour values (Fig. 6).

Binary calculations for the number of tones represented by common bit depths:

  • 1 bit (21) = 2 tones
  • 2 bits (22) = 4 tones
  • 3 bits (23) = 8 tones
  • 4 bits (24) = 16 tones
  • 8 bits (28) = 256 tones
  • 16 bits (216) = 65,536 tones
  • 24 bits (224) = 16.7 million tones

Note: The human eye cannot distinguish all 256 different levels of brightness in an 8 bits image, nor can they be successfully recorded or printed using ink-jet or laser printers.

If only 256 brightness levels are stretched to cover this entire range, there is not enough sensitivity to small variations to reveal detail in either bright or dark areas. Capturing images with higher bit depth, for instance 12 bits (4096 brightness levels, which is approximately the capability of a film camera), can record the data, but it cannot be viewed successfully on a display screen or in a print.

The threshold setting in bitonal scanning defines the point on a scale, ranging from 0 (black) to 255 (white), at which the gray values captured will be converted to black or white pixels. Note the effect of varying the threshold on typescript scanned at the same resolution on the same scanner (Fig. 7).

Dynamic range

the higher the dynamic range

DYNAMIC RANGE is the range of tonal difference between the lightest light and darkest dark of an image (Fig. 8). The higher the dynamic range, the more potential shades can be represented, although the dynamic range does not automatically correlate to the number of tones reproduced. For instance, high-contrast microfilm exhibits a broad dynamic range, but renders few tones. Dynamic range also describes a digital system's ability to reproduce tonal information. This capability is most important for continuous-tone documents that exhibit smoothly varying tones, and for photographs it may be the single most important aspect of image quality.

COMPRESSION is used to reduce image file size for storage, processing, and transmission. The file size for digital images can be quite large, taxing the computing and networking capabilities of many systems. All compression techniques abbreviate the string of binary code in an uncompressed image to a form of mathematical shorthand, based on complex algorithms. There are standard and proprietary compression techniques available. In general it is better to utilize a standard and broadly supported one, than a proprietary one that may offer more efficient compression and/or better quality, but which may not lend itself to long-term use or digital preservation strategies.

Compression

2 kinds of compression:

  • Lossless schemes: abbreviate binary code without discarding any information => when image is "decompressed" it is bit for bit identical to original (bitonal images)
  • Lossy schemes: utilize a means for averaging or discarding the least significant information, based on an understanding of visual perception. However, it may be extremely difficult to detect the effects of lossy compression; (typically used in tonal images).

Compression schemes can be further characterized as either lossless or lossy. Lossless schemes abbreviate the binary code without discarding any information, so that when the image is "decompressed" it is bit for bit identical to the original. Lossy schemes (Fig. 9), such as JPEG, utilize a means for averaging or discarding the least significant information, based on an understanding of visual perception. However, it may be extremely difficult to detect the effects of lossy compression, and the image may be considered "visually lossless." Lossless compression is most often used with bitonal scanning of textual material. Lossy compression is typically used with tonal images, and in particular continuous tone images where merely abbreviating the information will not result in any appreciable file savings.

Emerging compression schemes offer the capability of providing multi-resolution images from a single file, providing flexibility in the delivery and presentation of images to end users.

Spatial calibration

= process of assigning the number of pixels per unit length to an image

CALIBRATION: With computer analysis of digital images taken at the microscope, the images are represented in the form of picture elements, or pixels, which are small dots making up the image. In order to perform measurements, the image analysis tools need to know how many pixels there are in one unit length of the image. The unit length may be in millimeters, micrometers, or any other unit chosen according to the overall size of the image and its important components. The process of assigning the number of pixels per unit length to an image is known as calibration. Before undertaking any analysis, a system setup must be calibrated so that measurements such as length, perimeter, or area are calculated in a specific unit, rather than as number of pixels measured (Reedy and Kamboj 2004). The calibration for each setup depends on the magnification of the objective lens that is used to view the image under the microscope, the resolution under which the image is stored, and the digital camera/microscope combination that is being used to acquire the image. This calibration measure will then apply to all images taken with that combination. The appropriate calibration measure must be imported and applied to each image at the time of analysis, so it is crucial to store that information on the image itself, in the filename, or in an image database. Most digital microscope cameras include the capability of marking each image directly at the time of image capture with a scale bar. Where this was not done, a scale bar can be inserted after the fact with most image analysis software packages. It is important to include the scale bar in published reports rather than listing a magnification. Calculated magnifications apply to what the eye sees when looking into the microscope lens. In traditional work, magnifications had to be recalculated to adjust for publication size of photomicrographs. In image analysis work there are also many intermediate steps in which the digital image may be resized, which also would affect magnification. The original image as viewed under the microscope can be thought of as a rubber band being extended or contracted during photography, analysis, and final publication, making magnifications impossible to track. Thus most journals require scale bars in thin-section images, but do not list magnifications.

What is considered a good quality image can vary. For published descriptions of a material, an image that closely resembles what one sees when looking in the microscope is often desirable. However, image analysis may require enhancement of certain features, exaggeration of contrast, etc. Multiple versions of the same image may be produced during preprocessing, during various phases of analysis, and as publications are prepared; the original uncompressed image must always be retained. In order to keep all versions of an image organized and annotated, a good image database software package is a prerequisite for serious image analysis work (such as for example IQbase, published by MediaCybernetics).

IMAGE ENHANCEMENT OR PREPROCESSING: It is often necessary to enhance features of interest in order to perform an analysis. When a new project is initiated, identifying a reliable protocol for enhancement may be somewhat time-consuming. Once done, however, the same procedure can be applied routinely to similar materials undergoing the same analysis, and often routines can be set up to perform image enhancement or preprocessing steps automatically. One often needs to separate two or more different particle types, phases, or components, and then count or measure specific features of one or both populations. Before any measurement and analysis can be done, particles must be clearly visible, with high contrast between particles and matrix or between the components of interest. There are a variety of approaches to preprocessing to correct potential problems. For example, image contrast and brightness can be manipulated to emphasize particular features, and various algorithms can be applied such as an edge enhancement filter that highlights all edges and ignores particle masses (Schmitt 1993; Goins and Reedy 2000). Most comprehensive image analysis packages include all tools needed for preprocessing. For many thin sections, contrast enhancement is sufficient to improve the image enough for successful analysis. Some initial experimentation will help identify optimal preprocessing procedures for the material under study and the goals of the analysis.
